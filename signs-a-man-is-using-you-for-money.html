15 Signs A Man Is Using You For Money: Protect Yourself From Financial Abuse

Published: August 7, 2025
Reading Time: 8 min read
Category: Financial Abuse & Relationship Red Flags

Financial exploitation in relationships is a serious form of abuse that affects millions of people worldwide. When someone uses you for money, they're employing psychological manipulation tactics to control and exploit your financial resources. Understanding these warning signs can protect you from becoming trapped in a financially abusive relationship.

Research shows that financial abuse occurs in up to 99% of domestic violence cases, with 4.1 million women in the UK alone experiencing economic abuse from a current or former partner in the last year. This comprehensive guide will help you recognize the red flags and protect yourself from financial manipulation.

Understanding Financial Abuse in Relationships

Financial abuse, also known as economic abuse, occurs when someone uses money as a tool to gain power and control over their partner. According to financial psychologist Brad Klontz, "In a situation of financial abuse, there's a power imbalance in the relationship and somebody is leveraging money and resources to control the other person."

This form of abuse is particularly insidious because it often starts subtly, with behaviors that may initially seem caring or protective. However, these actions gradually escalate into controlling and coercive patterns that leave victims financially dependent and unable to leave the relationship.

15 Critical Warning Signs a Man Is Using You for Money

1. Complete Control Over Financial Decisions

One of the clearest signs of financial exploitation is when your partner takes complete control over all financial decisions without consulting you. This includes managing your income, expenses, and savings. He may claim it's to "reduce your stress" or because he's "better with money," but this behavior strips away your financial autonomy.

2. Restricting Access to Your Own Money

If you don't have access to your own bank accounts, credit cards, or cash, this is a major red flag. Financial abusers often control access to resources, making their partners dependent on them for even basic necessities like food, toiletries, or transportation.

3. Sabotaging Your Employment

Men who use women for money often interfere with their partner's ability to work. This might involve harassing you at work, showing up unexpectedly at your workplace, or preventing you from leaving the house for work. The goal is to make you financially dependent by eliminating your income source.

4. Opening Accounts or Using Credit Without Permission

Using your credit cards without permission, opening accounts in your name, or building up debts you didn't consent to are clear signs of financial abuse. This tactic not only exploits your resources but also damages your credit and financial standing.

5. Hiding Financial Information

If your partner conceals bank statements, bills, or other financial documents, he may be hiding financial abuse. Healthy relationships involve transparency about finances, especially when you're sharing expenses or living together.

6. Giving You an "Allowance" While Controlling Major Assets

Financial abusers often provide small amounts of spending money while maintaining control over larger assets and accounts. This creates the illusion of financial support while actually limiting your access to your own resources.

7. Pressuring You to Co-sign Financial Documents

If he frequently asks you to co-sign loans, credit applications, or other financial documents without fully explaining the terms, this is a manipulation tactic. Many abusers put assets in their name while leaving liabilities and debts in their partner's name.

8. Monitoring Your Spending Excessively

While it's normal for couples to discuss major purchases, excessive monitoring of every expense is a form of financial control. This includes questioning every purchase, demanding receipts, or becoming angry about normal expenses.

9. Preventing Education or Career Development

Financial abusers often discourage their partners from pursuing education or career advancement that could lead to financial independence. They may refuse to help with childcare during classes or create obstacles to prevent you from improving your earning potential.

10. Using Financial Threats as Punishment

Threatening to cut off financial support, cancel insurance, or withhold money as punishment for disagreements or perceived slights is a form of economic coercion designed to maintain control over your behavior.

11. Stealing or Selling Your Possessions

Taking your personal items, selling your belongings without permission, or using your possessions as collateral for his debts are clear signs of financial exploitation and disrespect for your property rights.

12. Filing False Tax Returns or Insurance Claims

If he files tax returns in your name without your knowledge or involvement, or makes insurance claims using your information, he's committing financial fraud that could have serious legal consequences for you.

13. Refusing to Work While Expecting Financial Support

While temporary unemployment can happen to anyone, a pattern of refusing to work while expecting you to support him financially, especially when combined with other controlling behaviors, indicates financial exploitation.

14. Creating Dependency Through Debt

Some abusers deliberately create financial dependency by encouraging their partners to quit work, move away from support networks, or make major financial commitments that require ongoing financial support from the abuser.

15. Using Financial Information for Threats or Blackmail

Threatening to ruin your credit, report you to authorities for financial issues he created, or use your financial vulnerabilities against you demonstrates the intersection of financial and emotional abuse.

The Psychology Behind Financial Manipulation

Financial abuse is rooted in the psychology of power and control. Research has identified six main manipulation tactics used by abusers: charm, silent treatment, coercion, reason, regression, and debasement. These tactics are designed to undermine your self-confidence and create deep dependency.

The abuser often begins with charm and seemingly caring behaviors, such as offering to manage finances to "help" you. Over time, this evolves into coercion and control. The psychological impact is profound – financial abuse deeply diminishes a victim's ability to stay safe and independent, making it extremely difficult to leave the relationship.

Protecting Yourself from Financial Exploitation

Establish Financial Independence

Maintain separate bank accounts and ensure you have access to your own financial resources. Even in committed relationships, having some financial independence is healthy and protective. Set up two-factor authentication on your banking information to ensure only you can access your finances.

Know Your Financial Situation

Stay informed about your credit score, bank statements, investment accounts, and loan agreements. Monitor your accounts regularly for unauthorized changes or transactions you didn't consent to. Knowledge is power when it comes to protecting yourself financially.

Set Clear Financial Boundaries

Establish boundaries early in relationships about how money will be managed. Decide together on spending limits that require joint consent, and maintain involvement in all major financial decisions. Healthy relationships involve transparency and mutual respect around finances.

Trust Your Instincts

If something feels wrong about your partner's financial behavior, trust your instincts. Financial abuse often starts with subtle control that gradually escalates. Don't dismiss your concerns or let your partner convince you that controlling behavior is normal or helpful.

Document Suspicious Activities

Keep records of any suspicious financial activities in a safe place your partner cannot access. This includes bank statements, canceled checks, and records of transactions you didn't consent to. This documentation can be crucial if you need to take legal action.

Building Healthy Financial Relationships

Healthy financial relationships are built on mutual respect, transparency, and shared decision-making. Partners should discuss financial goals openly, respect each other's financial autonomy, and make major financial decisions together. Both partners should have access to information about family finances and the ability to make reasonable purchases without excessive oversight.

Communication is key – regular budget meetings to assess progress toward shared goals, discussions about spending limits, and transparency about individual financial histories all contribute to a healthy financial relationship. Remember that financial boundaries aren't restrictions on love; they're protections that allow relationships to thrive.

When to Seek Help

If you recognize multiple warning signs in your relationship, it's important to seek support. Financial abuse can be a warning signal for other forms of abuse, as it occurs in nearly all cases of domestic violence. The isolation and dependency created by financial abuse can make it extremely difficult to leave without support.

Resources are available to help you create a safety plan, repair your financial security, and protect yourself and any children from further harm. The National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-7233) provides confidential support and can connect you with local resources.

Remember that financial abuse is never okay, regardless of your financial situation or relationship status. You deserve a relationship based on respect, trust, and mutual support – not manipulation and control.

Moving Forward: Recovery and Empowerment

Recovery from financial abuse takes time and often requires professional support. Focus on rebuilding your financial independence gradually, starting with small steps like opening your own bank account or monitoring your credit report. Consider working with a financial counselor who understands abuse dynamics to help you create a recovery plan.

Remember that recognizing these warning signs is the first step toward protecting yourself. Trust your instincts, maintain your financial independence, and don't hesitate to seek help if you're experiencing financial manipulation in your relationship. You have the right to financial safety and autonomy, and support is available to help you achieve it.
